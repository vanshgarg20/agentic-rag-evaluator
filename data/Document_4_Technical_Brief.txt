TECHNICAL BRIEF
AI Compliance Requirements: A Developer's Reference
Version 1.2 | Updated January 2024
Prepared by: Engineering Policy Working Group


PURPOSE

This brief provides a technical summary of compliance requirements under major AI regulations for engineering and product teams. It is intended as a practical reference, not legal advice.


SECTION 1: RISK CLASSIFICATION SUMMARY

Under the EU AI Act, AI systems are classified as follows:

Prohibited Systems
- Social scoring by public authorities
- Real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces (with exceptions)
- AI that exploits subconscious behavior to cause harm
- AI that exploits vulnerabilities of specific groups

High-Risk Systems (require conformity assessment)
- AI in critical infrastructure management
- AI in education (admissions, assessment, proctoring)
- AI in employment (recruitment, performance evaluation)
- AI in essential services (credit scoring, insurance)
- AI in law enforcement
- AI in migration and border control
- AI in administration of justice

Limited Risk (transparency obligations only)
- Chatbots must disclose they are AI
- Deepfakes must be labeled

Minimal Risk
- Everything else, including most consumer applications, spam filters, AI in video games


SECTION 2: TECHNICAL DOCUMENTATION REQUIREMENTS

For high-risk AI systems, the following documentation must be maintained and available for regulatory inspection:

1. General description of the AI system and its intended purpose
2. Description of system components and development process
3. Training data characteristics including data sources, collection methods, and preprocessing steps
4. Validation and testing methodology, including metrics used and test datasets
5. Description of human oversight mechanisms
6. Cybersecurity measures
7. Post-market monitoring plan

Documentation must be updated whenever the system undergoes significant changes. The definition of "significant change" is still being clarified in technical standards.


SECTION 3: US REQUIREMENTS (EXECUTIVE ORDER)

The October 2023 Executive Order established requirements primarily for developers of frontier models:

Frontier model threshold: Systems trained using computational resources exceeding 10^26 floating point operations.

Required actions for frontier model developers:
- Report to the federal government before deployment
- Share results of red-team safety testing
- Implement watermarking for AI-generated content where technically feasible

Sector-specific requirements are being developed by individual agencies. Engineering teams should monitor guidance from:
- NIST (general AI risk management)
- FTC (consumer-facing AI applications)
- EEOC (AI in employment contexts)
- OCC and CFPB (AI in financial services)


SECTION 4: DATA REQUIREMENTS

EU AI Act data requirements for high-risk systems:

Training data must be:
- Relevant and representative for the intended purpose
- Free from errors to the extent possible
- Complete with respect to the properties relevant to the use case

Particular attention required for:
- Potential biases that could lead to discrimination
- Gaps or shortcomings in training data

Note: The EU AI Act data requirements apply to training data. Separate GDPR obligations apply to any personal data used in training or inference.

China's generative AI regulations additionally require that training data not infringe third-party intellectual property rights. This requirement has no direct equivalent in current EU or US regulations, though copyright litigation in the US may produce similar practical constraints.


SECTION 5: PENALTIES REFERENCE

EU AI Act:
- Prohibited system violations: up to 35 million euros or 7% of global turnover
- High-risk system violations: up to 15 million euros or 3% of global turnover
- Providing incorrect information to authorities: up to 7.5 million euros or 1% of global turnover

US:
- No single federal AI law with defined penalties
- Existing laws (FTC Act, civil rights laws) apply; penalties vary by statute

China:
- Generative AI violations: warnings, fines, suspension of service


SECTION 6: TIMELINE REFERENCE

EU AI Act:
- 2024: Act enters into force; EU AI Office established
- 2025: Prohibitions on unacceptable risk systems apply
- 2026: Obligations for general-purpose AI models apply
- 2027: Full application including high-risk system requirements

US Executive Order:
- Ongoing: Agency rulemaking and guidance development
- No fixed legislative timeline

China:
- Generative AI regulations: in force since August 2023


NOTES AND KNOWN INCONSISTENCIES

The penalty figures cited in Section 5 reflect the final published text of the EU AI Act. Earlier drafts and some secondary sources cite different figures (e.g., 30 million euros / 6% for the highest tier). Teams should rely on the final published regulation.

The timeline in Section 6 reflects our best reading of the legislation. Official EU guidance on exact application dates for specific provisions is still being published.

This document will be updated as implementing regulations and technical standards are finalized.
