MEMORANDUM

TO: Members, Coalition for Responsible AI Development (CRAID)
FROM: Policy & Advocacy Team
DATE: February 2024
RE: Recommended Positions on Pending AI Legislation


This memo summarizes our recommended positions ahead of the Q1 advocacy meetings. Please review before the coalition call on Feb 22.


BACKGROUND

Legislation affecting AI development is advancing simultaneously in multiple jurisdictions. Our members operate across the US, EU, UK, and Canada, creating a complex compliance environment. This memo focuses on the three most immediate policy developments.


ISSUE 1: EU AI ACT IMPLEMENTATION

Status: Passed. Implementation timeline running 2024-2027.

Our position: Support the EU AI Act in principle while pushing for clearer technical standards in the implementation phase. The current high-risk classification criteria remain ambiguous in several areas particularly around AI used in HR and recruitment processes.

Recommended ask: Engage with the EU AI Office during the standards-drafting phase to ensure technical requirements are workable for smaller developers, not just large enterprises.

Internal note: Two of our members have flagged that the conformity assessment requirements could cost upward of $2 million per product line. This figure has not been externally verified and should not be cited publicly.


ISSUE 2: US EXECUTIVE ORDER ON AI

Status: Active. Agency rulemaking ongoing.

The October 2023 Executive Order directed agencies to produce guidance across a wide range of AI applications. NIST released its AI Risk Management Framework update in January 2024.

Our position: Broadly supportive. The voluntary framework approach preserves flexibility. However, we are concerned that the requirement for safety reporting on frontier models (defined as those trained using more than 10^26 FLOPs) may create precedents for more prescriptive disclosure requirements.

We do not yet have a formal position on the watermarking provisions. Staff will circulate a briefing note by end of February.


ISSUE 3: UK AI REGULATION

The UK government has confirmed it will not pursue primary AI legislation in the near term, instead relying on existing regulators to apply their mandates to AI systems.

Our position: We support the UK's principles-based approach as a useful counterpoint to the EU's prescriptive model. The UK's approach could serve as a template for jurisdictions that want to signal AI governance without imposing compliance costs.

Note: The UK position may shift following the general election expected in mid-2024. Members with significant UK operations should monitor this closely.


COORDINATION NOTE

We are coordinating with the Global AI Policy Forum on a joint submission to the UN Advisory Body on AI, due March 15. Members who wish to contribute should contact the policy team by Feb 28.
